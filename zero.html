True ZERO emissions cruises

No SOx, NOx, or noise

We need to PROTECT our seas, oceans, and local communities.

The main pollution coming from the cruise industry is BOTH particle emissions and NOISE.

Let us demonstrate how successful, fun and enjoyable cruises can run on zero emissions and benefit local communities.

WHY?

01

PARTICLE EMISSIONS

SOx, NOx, and sewage

The two major air pollutants from cruising activity are SOx (Sulur oxides) and NOx (Nitrogen oxides), which have an impact on greenhouse gas levels and climate change.

All cruise ships, big and small, in the market today, emit particulate matter. Even sail yachts, as they require an engine to maneuver around the port, and to sail when the wind is not blowing, or when trying to escape a storm. Or solar catamarans, as they use generators to extend range.

According to Forbes, German watchdog Nabu surveyed 77 cruise ships and found that all but one used toxic heavy fuel oil, the dirtiest of all fuels. Most of them even fail to adhere to their own air quality safety standards.

Standing on the deck of a cruise ship is similar to being in one of the world’s most polluted cities. 10% of air pollution in Marseilles can be directly contributed to the shipping industry. It is estimated that over 50,000 Europeans die prematurely every year as a result of shipping-based pollution. A single cruise ship can emit as much pollution as 700 trucks and as much particulate matter as a million cars. The US Environment Protection Agency (EPA) estimates that an average cruise ship emits the same sulfur oxide contaminants in a single day, as 13 million cars. Scrubbers wash cheap fuel in order to meet environmental standards, but then discharge the pollutants collected directly into the ocean.

02

SOUND POLLUTION

Noise

According to UBS "Sea beyond blue", noise might not be the first threat that comes to mind when considering dangers to the oceans. But, water is an especially efficient sound conductor. Research shows that levels of anthropogenic (human-generated) noise have doubled every decade for the past 60 years in some regions, which leads to stranding of whales and dolphins, hearing damage in marine animals, and fleeing valuable habitats.

For example, the protected migration corridor for whales and dolphins along the Spanish coast between the Alboran Sea and the Gulf of Lion is one of the

four core areas of particular importance to noise-sensitive marine mammal species, as identified in a report by OceanCare to the Agreement on the Conservation of Cetaceans in the Mediterranean Sea.
